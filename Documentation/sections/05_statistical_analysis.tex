\chapter{Statistical Analysis}
\label{chap:statistical_analysis}
\section{Analytical Aims and Data Provenance}
This chapter analyses the outcomes produced by the architectural design in \Cref{chap:arch_design} and the experimental design in \Cref{chap:experimental_design}. The primary aim is to answer the research questions outlined in \Cref{sec:research_questions}. The analysis will focus on the following key questions:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:}, leftmargin=*]
    \item How do \emph{overlap ratio} and \emph{background noise} affect ASR accuracy under controlled synthetic conditions?
    \item How do publicly available ASR models compare across different overlap and noise operating points?
    \item To what extent do trends observed on controlled \emph{synthetic mixtures} transfer to real conversational recordings with natural overlap?
    \item Does the proposed double-stream serialisation metric provide a more suitable evaluation than ordinary WER when a two-speaker reference is compared with a single-stream hypothesis?
\end{enumerate}
The analysis uses the top-level result files generated by the evaluation pipelines described in \Cref{chap:arch_design}, which contain the WER, DSS-WER, ORC-WER and other relevant metrics for each ASR model across all experimental conditions. 

Two analysis scopes are kept separate:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A 700-clip matched subset for each of the 4 ASR models, defined as
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{split}
        \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{model}} ={}&
        \{\mathrm{SNR}=7.4,\ \mathrm{noise}=T,\ \mathrm{OVR}\in\{0.00,0.14,0.20,0.40\}\}\\
        &\cup
        \{\mathrm{OVR}=0.14,\ \mathrm{noise}=T,\ \mathrm{SNR}\in\{\mathrm{clean},7.4,0,-5\}\}.
        \end{split}
    \end{equation}
    Each combination of factors contains 100 clips, for a total of 700 per model, as shown in \Cref{fig:stat_cross_model_scope_support}. 
    \item the full \texttt{wav2vec-2.0} result file, which contains 4800 raw rows. Since the clean SNR condition is repeated once for each noise-type label despite containing the same no-noise audio, the full-scope \texttt{wav2vec-2.0} analysis collapses these clean duplicates to one effective sample per base mixture. This gives 4000 effective rows: 400 clean rows and 3600 noisy rows.   
\end{enumerate}

The first scope allows direct comparison of the 4 ASR models across the same set of clips. The reason for not using the full 4000-row effective sweep for all models is due to runtime and computational constraints. This is suitable for fair model ranking but should not be used to make claims about the effects of SNR-overlap combinations across all models. The second scope allows a more comprehensive analysis of the effects of overlap and noise on the ASR performance of \texttt{wav2vec-2.0}, while avoiding triple-counting the clean condition.

\section{Variable Selection}
\subsection{Dependent Variables}
The primary dependent variable is the clip-level Word Error Rate (WER). The reason for choosing WER is that it is the de facto metric for ASR evaluation \cite{sasindran2023hevalnewhybridevaluation} and is available for all four models, including segmented and non-segmented hypotheses.

ORC-WER and DSS-WER are also available, where ORC-WER is only available for the segmented hypothesis (from \texttt{faster-whisper} and \texttt{WhisperX}) and DSS-WER is available for all models. They act as secondary metrics to provide additional insights into the performance. They are valuable as they are designed to handle different situations. ORC-WER is designed to represent the speaker-agnostic lexical accuracy of the segmented hypothesis. In contrast, DSS-WER is designed to evaluate the specific case of comparing a two-stream reference with a single-stream hypothesis, which is the case for this evaluation. However, since it is proposed in this dissertation, it is important to evaluate its behaviour and suitability before applying it as the primary metric. Therefore, WER is the most suitable and thus chosen as the primary metric for model comparison. At the same time, DSS-WER will be tested for its suitability and insights as a secondary metric.

\subsection{Explanatory Variables}
The main explanatory variables are signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), overlap ratio (OVR), noise type and ASR model. SNR and OVR are the two main factors of interest in ASR evaluation: noise and overlap. Overlap is the central phenomenon being investigated in this dissertation. Noise type is included as it might interact with the effects of SNR and OVR. The ASR model is included because it is important to understand how different models perform under the same conditions and whether the effects of SNR and OVR differ across models.

The speaker count is held constant at 2 for all clips. This makes OVR more interpretable as a controlled measure of two-speaker overlap rather than a more complex interaction between overlap and speaker count. The clip duration is also set to 60 seconds, which supports comparability across clips and models, providing enough speech content while keeping inference time manageable.

\section{Statistical Methods}
All reported results use the (clip ID, model) pair as the unit of analysis. The design is a factorial design with repeated measures, where the same base audio is used across different SNR and noise type conditions; cross-model analysis uses the same clip IDs system across models. For this reason, the analysis will focus on matched condition summaries and comparisons.

WER is non-negative and often skewed, so the mean is reported as the primary summary statistic. Still, the median and standard deviation are also reported to provide a more complete picture.

\subsection{Effect of Overlap Ratio}
\Cref{fig:stat_wav2vec2_wer_vs_ovr} and \Cref{tab:wav2vec2_snr_overlap_mean_wer} show the general trends of WER against the overlap ratio. The results show a monotonic increase in WER as the overlap ratio increases. The marginal mean WER rises from 0.288 at OVR 0.00 to 0.563 at OVR 0.40, as shown in \Cref{tab:wav2vec2_marginal_wer}.

\Cref{fig:stat_wav2vec2_delta_vs_snr_panel} shows the delta WER $\Delta_\text{WER, 0\% OVR}$ against SNR, where
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_\text{WER, 0\% OVR} = \text{WER}_{\mathrm{SNR}, \mathrm{OVR}} - \text{WER}_{\mathrm{SNR}, 0\%}.
\end{equation}
This isolates the effect of overlap as SNR changes. The results show that the $\Delta_\text{WER, 0\% OVR}$ increases as the SNR decreases, which suggests that the effect of overlap is more substantial when SNR is low (noisier conditions). The $\Delta_\text{WER, 0\% OVR}$ diverges across different overlap ratio conditions as the SNR decreases, which matches the observation from \Cref{fig:stat_wav2vec2_delta_vs_ovr_panel} that the effect of overlap becomes more notable as the SNR decreases.

\subsection{Effect of Signal-to-Noise Ratio}
\Cref{fig:stat_wav2vec2_wer_vs_snr} and \Cref{tab:wav2vec2_snr_overlap_mean_wer} show the general trends of WER against SNR. The results show a considerable increase in WER as the SNR decreases (i.e., the signal becomes noisier). The mean WER rises from 0.224 in the clean condition to 0.588 at -5 dB SNR, as shown in \Cref{tab:wav2vec2_marginal_wer}.

\Cref{fig:stat_wav2vec2_delta_vs_ovr_panel} shows the delta WER $\Delta_\text{WER, clean SNR}$ against overlap ratio, where
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_\text{WER, clean SNR} = \text{WER}_{\mathrm{SNR}, \mathrm{OVR}} - \text{WER}_{\mathrm{clean}, \mathrm{OVR}}.
\end{equation}
This isolates the effect of noise as the overlap ratio changes. The results show that the $\Delta_\text{WER, clean SNR}$ decreases as the overlap ratio increases, which suggests that the effect of noise is more substantial when the overlap ratio is low, and decreases as the overlap ratio increases. The $\Delta_\text{WER, clean SNR}$ starts to converge across different SNR conditions as the overlap ratio increases, which suggests that the effect of noise starts to become dominated by the effect of overlap as the overlap ratio increases.

To summarise the trend as a continuous estimating equation, SNR is converted to an amplitude noise ratio
\begin{equation}
    z(\mathrm{SNR}) =
    \begin{cases}
        0, & \mathrm{clean}\;(\mathrm{SNR}=\infty),\\
        10^{-\mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{dB}}/20}, & \text{otherwise},
    \end{cases}
\end{equation} 
changing SNR from logarithmic to linear scale, making SNR a continuous variable. Fitting a least-squares surface to the full \texttt{wav2vec-2.0} scope gives
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\mathrm{WER}} =
    0.020 + 1.015\,\mathrm{OVR} + 0.281\,z - 0.338\,(\mathrm{OVR}\times z).
\end{equation}
The interaction term is retained because the noise penalty is not constant across overlap ratios. This equation should be interpreted as an estimate of the mean WER averaged over noise type: it gives a strong fit to the 16 SNR--OVR condition means ($R^2=0.986$, MAE $=0.018$ WER), where
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{R}^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathrm{\{\mathrm{SNR} \times \mathrm{OVR}\}}}(\mathrm{WER}_i - \widehat{\mathrm{WER}}_i)^2}{\sum_{i \in \mathrm{\{\mathrm{SNR} \times \mathrm{OVR}\}}}(\mathrm{WER}_i - \overline{\mathrm{WER}})^2},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{MAE} = \frac{1}{16}\sum_{i \in \mathrm{\{\mathrm{SNR} \times \mathrm{OVR}\}}}|\mathrm{WER}_i - \widehat{\mathrm{WER}}_i|,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{R}^2$ is the coefficient of determination, $\mathrm{MAE}$ is the mean absolute error, and $\overline{\mathrm{WER}}$ is the mean WER across the 16 conditions. 
The clip-level fit is lower ($R^2=0.362$, MAE $=0.171$ WER) because individual clips vary substantially in lexical content and acoustic difficulty. The result shows that the OVR dominates the WER increase as it has a larger coefficient than the noise term. However, the negative interaction term indicates an antagonistic relationship between noise and overlap, suggesting the marginal effect of one degradation factor is smaller when the other is already severe.

\subsection{Summary}
The results show a positive relationship between WER and the overlap ratio, and between WER and noise (noisy conditions have higher WER than the clean condition). The effect of the overlap ratio is more substantial in noisier conditions, while the effect of noise is stronger in lower overlap conditions. The fitted equation suggests that the effect of overlap ratio is more significant than that of noise, but they interact in an antagonistic manner, reducing each other's impact on WER.

\subsection{Overall Comparison}
\Cref{tab:cross_model_wer_first} records the WER, ORC-WER and DSS-WER for the four ASR models on the matched 700-clip subset. The results show that \texttt{faster-whisper} has the lowest mean WER of  0.159, while \texttt{Parakeet} has the highest mean WER of 0.503, with a substantial gap between the second-highest model \texttt{wav2vec-2.0} of 0.228. The ORC-WER and DSS-WER values are generally consistent with the WER ranking, with \texttt{faster-whisper} having the lowest DSS-WER. However, the ORC-WER values for \texttt{faster-whisper} are higher than \texttt{WhisperX} even though \texttt{faster-whisper} has a lower WER. This suggests that \texttt{faster-whisper} might have a better lexical accuracy but worse segmentation accuracy than \texttt{WhisperX}.

\Cref{fig:stat_cross_model_wer_trends} shows the WER trends against overlap ratio and SNR for the four ASR models. Both show the same ranking of the models across conditions, with $\texttt{faster-whisper} < \texttt{WhisperX} < \texttt{wav2vec-2.0} < \texttt{Parakeet}$, which is consistent with the overall WER ranking in \Cref{tab:cross_model_wer_first}. The gap between the models remains relatively consistent as the overlap ratio increases, but the gap decreases as the SNR decreases. It is interesting to note that \texttt{Parakeet} 's performance is counterintuitively better as noise increases, which might be due to differences in the pretraining data and architecture. In addition, it has a dip in WER (slight increase in WER) from 0.14 OVR to 0.20 OVR. Neither of these behaviours is observed in the other three models, which might suggest that \texttt{Parakeet} and the architectural design (conformer and transducer-based architecture) are more resistant to noise. This is an interesting finding that warrants further experimentation and analysis, but it is not the main focus of this dissertation, so it will not be further investigated in this chapter.

Hence, the choice of model depends on the specific requirements of the application, such as the acceptable WER and available computational resources.

\section{RQ4: Evaluation of DSS-WER}
\label{sec:rq4_evaluation_of_dss_wer}

This section evaluates how DSS-WER behaves compared to ordinary WER, and whether it shows the expected behaviour based on the experimental design in \Cref{sec:dss_wer_algorithm} and \Cref{sec:t3_dss_wer_hypothesis_testing}. This analysis uses all successful synthetic results from all four ASR models. This means, 700 clips for \texttt{faster-whisper}, \texttt{WhisperX}, and \texttt{Parakeet}, and 4000 clips for \texttt{wav2vec-2.0}. The results are split into non-overlapping clips $\mathrm{OVR} = 0.00$ and overlapping clips $\mathrm{OVR} > 0.00$.

For each clip-model pair, the difference is measured as
\begin{equation}
    d_i = \mathrm{DSS\text{-}WER}_i - \mathrm{WER}_i.
\end{equation}
Negative values of $d_i$ mean that DSS-WER gives a lower error estimate than WER. Following the experimental design in \Cref{sec:t3_dss_wer_hypothesis_testing}, the permutation tests use 10,000 random sign flips of the paired differences. The non-overlap test is two-sided because DSS-WER should ideally match WER exactly when there is no interleaving. The overlap test is one-sided in the lower-tail direction because the expected effect is that DSS-WER should be less than WER when there is interleaving. The confidence intervals are 95\% bootstrap intervals for the mean paired difference, based on 10,000 resamples.

That means even though the control condition doesn't show a large mean difference, the approximation is not perfect and still overestimates the error by a small amount on average. The model exception is \texttt{wav2vec-2.0}, where the confidence interval includes zero, and the p-value is not significant ($p=0.277$), which suggests that DSS-WER is a very close approximation to WER. This suggests that the DSS-WER algorithm is not just simply giving a lower error estimate but is actually able to find the correct path when there is no interleaving. 

\Cref{tab:dss_wer_overlap_test} shows the advantage of DSS-WER over WER in the overlapping condition, with mean paired differences of $-0.0164$ overall, a confidence interval that does not include zero ($[-0.0173, -0.0154]$) and a p-value of $<0.001$. This suggests that DSS-WER can significantly reduce the overestimation of error under overlapping speech for \texttt{wav2vec-2.0}. However, for \texttt{Parakeet}, the mean paired difference is positive at $+0.0116$ with a confidence interval of $[0.0086, 0.0147]$ and a p-value of 1.000, which indicates that DSS-WER actually gives a higher estimate than WER on average. This might be because \texttt{Parakeet} performs much worse than the other models, so the beam search algorithm of DSS-WER might be more likely to miss the correct path in the search space. In the case of a correct hypothesis, there are paths with significantly lower error, so the algorithm can easily narrow down to a few paths. On the other hand, if the hypothesis completely missed the reference, there might be many equally bad paths. Since the algorithm has a fixed beam width, it might not be able to explore all paths and thus miss the correct path, leading to a higher error rate. This suggests that the advantage of DSS-WER over WER under overlapping might depend on the hypothesis's accuracy.

\Cref{tab:dss_wer_direction_counts} gives the clip-level direction of the paired differences. In the non-overlapping condition, about 63.3\% of pairs have DSS-WER equal to WER, supporting the use of non-overlapping clips as a control. In the overlapping condition, about 79\% of the pairs have DSS-WER less than or equal to WER, which supports the expected advantage of DSS-WER under overlapping speech. However, it also suggests that 21\% of the pairs have DSS-WER greater than WER, which should be kept in mind when interpreting the results.

\subsection{Synthetic Examples}
\label{sec:synthetic_examples}
\Cref{fig:dss_wer_diff_exp} demonstrate the two example where the WER overestimate the actual error while DSS-WER gives a more accurate estimate. They are all due to the interleaving of the two speakers who is not correctly represented in the reference transcript. Even though the hypothesis is correct in terms of the chronological order of every word, but since reference is segmented by sentences, the interleaving of the two speakers is not correctly represented in the reference, which leads to a high WER. However, DSS-WER can find the correct path that matches the hypothesis and thus gives a much lower error estimate.

\subsection{Summary}
Overall, the results suggest that DSS-WER gives a statistically significant but small overestimation of error in the non-overlapping condition, and a statistically significant reduction in error in the overlapping condition, especially for \texttt{wav2vec-2.0}. In further applications of DSS-WER, it is important to keep in mind that the advantage of DSS-WER under overlapping might depend on the hypothesis's accuracy. On the other hand, the computational cost of DSS-WER is considerably higher than that of WER, so it might not be suitable for all application. It should be used in scenarios where the benefits of accurate evaluation outweigh the computational costs.For future use, it might be worth revisiting WER if there is no overlap. On the other hand, to improve the algorithm, it might be worth considering doing some hyperparameter tuning of the beam search algorithm, such as increasing beam width or lookahead steps or changing the heuristic, to see if it can further reduce the error under overlapping speech. 

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:rq_summary_of_findings}
This chapter has presented the results of the statistical analysis for all 4 research questions. For RQ1, the results show that WER increases with higher OVR and lower SNR, which is consistent with the expected trends. For RQ2, the results show that \texttt{faster-whisper} has the best WER but the worst inference time, while \texttt{wav2vec-2.0} has the best inference time but a higher WER, and \texttt{WhisperX} is a balance between the two. For RQ3, the results show that the synthetic ranking transfers only partially to real data, with \texttt{Parakeet} performing best among the four ASR models. For RQ4, the results show that DSS-WER provides a statistically significant advantage over WER under the overlapping condition but shows a small overestimation in the non-overlapping condition.
